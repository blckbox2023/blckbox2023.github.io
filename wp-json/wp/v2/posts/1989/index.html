This story isn\u2019t about Ukraine\u2019s top-level corrupt officials, the ones making headlines while enriching themselves off state resources. It\u2019s about regional operators \u2014 local fixers, working hand in hand with municipal officials to drain defense budgets while the country\u2019s attention is focused elsewhere.<\/em><\/p>\n\n\n\n

And that\u2019s exactly why the case of Dmytro Shlimovych from Ivano-Frankivsk matters. Because it shows a pattern: he is not an exception. There are likely not dozens, but hundreds or even thousands of such petty crooks across Ukraine. And together, their schemes may be causing more damage to the country at war than the high-profile scandals investigated by NABU.<\/em><\/p>\n\n\n\n

Stealing during wartime became much easier after the Cabinet of Ministers adopted Resolution<\/a> No. 1178 on October 12, 2022. This decree approved \u201cspecial procedures for public procurement of goods, works, and services\u201d during martial law and 90 days after it ends. In practice, it opened the door for every state institution and local authority to bypass competition and carry out purchases in the shortest possible time. The logic was simple: the army needs supplies fast, without the delays of lengthy tenders.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

But predictably, corrupt officials and businessmen rushed to exploit these rules. Especially when they gained access to hundreds of millions of hryvnias allocated for defense programs.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

How Money Laundering Thrives in the Regions Through the State Program \u201cDefense of Ukraine\u201d<\/strong><\/h2>\n\n\n\n

For example, in 2024 the government decided to prepare children for war by introducing a new subject in schools and vocational colleges called \u201cDefense of Ukraine\u201d. Pilot centers were created in several regions, with dozens of schools being re-equipped with modern technology, so students could practice military skills. For this purpose, the government allocated<\/a> \u20b41.65 billion in subsidies to the regions.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In Ivano-Frankivsk region, 64 such centers were established \u2014 in 53 lyceums and 11 vocational schools. They were provided with equipment worth more than \u20b496.4 million. According to DOZORRO Transparency International Ukraine, this was the third-largest amount spent nationwide (after Kyiv and Lviv region) to teach children the basics of warfare. The bulk of the money in Ivano-Frankivsk was funneled through just three procurements by the regional Department of Education and Science, almost all of it going to a little-known local company, Optomix LLC, created only shortly before.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The company was registered on April 4, 2024, with a charter capital of just \u20b410,000. Yet by November of the same year, it had already landed its first major contract, supplying an interactive laser shooting range<\/a> worth more than \u20b45.5 million. Another bidder, Strata22 LLC (now MKS LLC), offered a price half a million hryvnias lower and complained<\/a> that the tender requirements had been deliberately tailored to fit Optomix, but its complaints went nowhere.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

A month later, the department purchased<\/a> another 64 sets of training kits from Optomix for almost \u20b47.5 million \u2014 this time in a tender with no competition at all, where Optomix was the sole participant. And in May 2025, the firm received yet another contract, this time worth \u20b411.5 million, for supplying<\/a> mock Kalashnikov rifles.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

It was this last procurement that raised the most suspicion. Even though Optomix formally had a competitor, sole proprietor Serhiy Rymar, the Department of Education and Science still ended up buying the rifles at inflated prices: 91 Kalashnikov training models in 7.62 caliber and 59 in 5.45 caliber, each priced at \u20b464,165. The total paid was \u20b411,549,700 including VAT.<\/p>\n\n\n\n
